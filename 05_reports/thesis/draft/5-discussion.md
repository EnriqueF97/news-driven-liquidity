# Chapter 5 - Discussion

This chapter interprets the empirical findings of Chapter 4, situates them against the prior work of Chapter 2, and states the limitations of the study. The discussion is organized around the two research questions (the lag structure of the news-liquidity response, RQ1, and its directional asymmetry, RQ2), followed by the methodological contributions of the two-phase design and the limitations that qualify the conclusions.

## 5.1 Lag structure of the news-liquidity response (RQ1)

RQ1 asks at what temporal lag news events makes their strongest effect on WTI liquidity. Two distinct analyses converge on the same answer.

- **Phase 1's lag OLS** (§4.2.3) places the peak of the bearish news effect on log_volume at +6 hours, with a secondary trace at +12 hours.
- **Phase 2's TFT v2** corroborates this through two independent diagnostics (§4.3.7.5): the per-horizon error curve improves most over persistence in the +6 to +12 hour window, and the encoder attention, disaggregated by sentiment direction, peaks at -6 hours for bearish-sentiment hours, aligning with the lag OLS peak.

The fact that a regression coefficient estimated on a single sentiment value at a fixed lag and a deep-learning forecaster integrating a 48-hour window of many features independently identify the same window (_+6 to +12 hours_) is the strongest evidence the thesis offers for RQ1.

The substantive reading is that news information does not reprice liquidity instantaneously but propagates over a multi-hour window. A purely contemporaneous response would place the peak at lag zero, and a market that fully and immediately absorbed news would show no predictable structure at +6 to +12 hours.

Instead, the response builds and persists across the trading session and into the next hour, consistent with gradual information diffusion and staggered trader reaction rather than a single immediate repricing. The TFT attention lands this picture: a strong recency component at -1 hour (the most recent news is always the most informative for the immediate forecast) coexists with a longer integration window for bearish news specifically.

The market appears to **take longer** to work through news that implies a _downward_ price move than news that implies an _upward_ one, a directional nuance in the temporal structure that Phase 1's average-lag estimate cannot resolve but that the TFT's direction-conditioned attention makes visible.

This lag structure is consistent with the premise established in the literature (§2.2) that news sentiment moves trading activity and not only prices [CITE: Tetlock 2007]. The contribution here is one of resolution: prior work operating at **daily** frequency cannot observe an intraday +6 to +12 hour propagation window, which the **hourly** design of this thesis is built to detect. That news effects concentrate at high frequency, largely within hours of release, is itself established in the literature [CITE: Ederington & Lee 1993]. The finding also frames the result in practical terms, an hourly-resolution monitor of WTI-relevant news carries information about liquidity that is most actionable not in the same hour but over the following half to full trading day.

## 5.2 Directional asymmetry of the response (RQ2)

RQ2 asks whether bearish news produces a different liquidity response than comparable bullish news. Phase 1's lag OLS (§4.2.3) answers yes: the coefficient on bearish sentiment exceeds the coefficient on bullish sentiment at the dominant lags, a robust directional asymmetry that is the thesis' primary evidence for RQ2. This is a statement about marginal sensitivity, how much an increment of bearish rather than bullish sentiment moves volume, holding the other fixed.

Phase 2's TFT v2 does not reproduce this asymmetry in its point predictions: across the sixteen bearish-versus-bullish comparisons of predicted volume (§4.3.7.6), none is significant. It would be a mistake to read this as a failed replication or as an underpowered test, because the two analyses estimate different quantities.

The lag OLS compares marginal coefficients, a slope holding other variables fixed; the TFT test compares the average predicted volume of bearish against bullish hours, an unconditional difference of group means. A larger marginal sensitivity to bearish news can coexist with near-equal average volume across bearish and bullish hours, for instance when bullish hours cluster in higher-baseline-volume periods. The null is not a power artifact: the two groups each hold hundreds of samples, the result is robust to the sentiment threshold across the range from 0.0 to 0.5, and the target itself is near-symmetric, since in the actual test data the future volume of bearish and bullish hours is statistically indistinguishable at the 1 to 6 hour horizons. The model even reproduces the one genuine difference, a bullish-above-bearish gap at +12 hours, faithfully. It is tracking a near-symmetric ground truth, not washing out a signal that is present.

A second and deeper reason the two phases point differently is that they define sentiment differently (§4.3.8.1). FinBERT scores the surface financial tone of an article, whereas the Haiku sentiment_score is prompted for the net directional impact on the WTI price. The two agree only moderately (Pearson 0.54) and diverge in sign on exactly one kind of news: geopolitical events.

> A headline such as "Russian Sanctions Drive Oil Prices Higher" is negative in tone (sanctions, conflict) but bullish in price (a supply threat lifts crude), so FinBERT labels it bearish while Haiku labels it bullish.

This geopolitical news is precisely the high-volume, high-risk-premium subset that dominates the liquidity response. Under Phase 1's tone-based definition it falls in the bearish bucket, reinforcing the bearish over bullish asymmetry; under Phase 2's price-based definition it falls in the bullish bucket, which is why the TFT's price-conditioned test finds no bearish over bullish gap and even a mild reversal. The apparent tension between the phases is therefore not a contradiction but a consequence of measuring bearishness two different ways.

**For the TFT it is not a matter of negative versus positive news, but of the _risk premium_ the news implies.**

What the TFT actually relies on clarifies this even more. Its most important features (§4.3.7.4) are VIX, the supply and demand channels, and a set of geopolitical entity flags dominated by actors around the Strait of Hormuz (Oman, Iran, Qatar). None of these is a bearish signal in the price sense; they are markers of risk and salience. The entities the model weights most are neutral-to-bullish in price impact but flag hours of elevated volume and risk premium regardless of direction. The asymmetry the model has learned is therefore not bearish over bullish but risk-and-salience over baseline: WTI liquidity responds to how much a piece of news raises uncertainty about supply, not to whether it implies a higher or lower price.

The answer to RQ2 is consequently conditional on how sentiment is defined and on which quantity is estimated. There is a robust bearish over bullish asymmetry in the marginal sensitivity of volume to negative-tone news (Phase 1), and this remains the thesis' primary answer. It does not show as a difference in the level of predicted volume between price-bearish and price-bullish hours (Phase 2), because the volume-driving geopolitical news is price-bullish and because the model organizes its predictions around risk and salience rather than price direction. Both readings are correct within their own framing, and stating the asymmetry precisely requires naming the sentiment construct and the estimand it refers to.

## 5.3 Methodological contributions

Beyond its answers to the two research questions, the thesis makes four methodological contributions, each arising from a concrete problem encountered in the work rather than proposed in the abstract.

**Channel decomposition as a validation substrate.** The decomposition of sentiment into orthogonal supply, demand, and risk-premium channels (§3.3.4), grounded in the structural decomposition of oil-price shocks [CITE: Kilian 2009], was introduced to solve a specific failure: the composite sentiment_score disagreed across model families (Haiku versus GPT-5.5) on high-magnitude geopolitical events (§4.3.4). Recalibrating on the channels raised cross-model agreement on the composite from 0.39 to 0.88, with channel correlations of 0.94, 0.96, and 0.82. The channels therefore play a dual role: they are higher-resolution features for the TFT, and, in the absence of human ground truth, cross-model agreement on them serves as a defensible validation signal for the extraction [CITE: Gilardi et al. 2023; Zheng et al. 2023].

**Tone versus price impact.** A tone classifier and a price-reasoning LLM are not a coarse and a fine version of the same measurement; they measure different constructs (§4.3.8.1, §5.2). FinBERT reports the surface financial tone of an article, while the LLM performs the asset-specific causal step, from a supply threat to a higher crude price, that a tone classifier cannot. The geopolitical sign-flip is the empirical demonstration. The lesson generalizes beyond this thesis: for an asset-specific task, sentiment must be defined relative to the asset and its causal mechanism, not as generic positive-or-negative tone, and a study that conflates the two can reach opposite conclusions depending on which it happens to use.

**LLM-as-filter for noisy news corpora.** Phase 1's inclusion rule was a brittle regex heuristic on the article body (§3.4.1); Phase 2 replaced it with the LLM's own judgment of whether an article is materially relevant (`usable`), refined to `usable_strict` by additionally requiring at least a non-zero channel score (§4.3.3). The LLM filter is semantically grounded where the regex is lexical, and the comparison of the two (§4.3.3) shows where each fails. For web-scraped news, where relevance is a matter of meaning rather than keywords, a semantically grounded filter is a practical and transferable alternative to keyword heuristics.

**Phase-based progression as a reproducible template.** The two-phase design is itself a contribution. A simple, interpretable baseline (FinBERT features with OLS) answers the research questions with classical statistics whose assumptions are transparent; a richer extraction with a more expressive model (LLM channels with a TFT) then both corroborates the baseline and enriches the analytical vocabulary. Neither phase is discarded: the second does not supersede the first but triangulates it, and each one has limitations on their own. This progression is a reusable template for empirical news-impact studies that want both interpretability and expressiveness without sacrificing either.

## 5.4 Limitations

Several limitations qualify the conclusions and delimit their scope.

**Inter-model calibration is weaker than human ground truth.** The LLM features are validated by cross-model agreement (Haiku versus GPT-5.5, §4.3.4) rather than against expert human annotation, which was outside the resource scope (§3.7). Cross-family agreement is informative, two models built by different developers converging on the same reading is evidence of something real, but it is not a gold standard: both models can share the same blind spot, and agreement does not guarantee correctness. The calibration sample is also small (_n=30_) by design, enough to surface systematic disagreements but not to estimate population agreement precisely.

**Regime extrapolation on price_range.** The most concrete modeling limitation is the failure of TFT v2 on price_range in the war regime (§4.3.7.3). Trained only on the pre-war period, the model reverts toward the historical mean when asked to predict the high-volatility war regime it never saw, underperforming even a naive persistence baseline. This is a clean illustration of a model informationally constrained by its training window, and a warning that the aggregate liquidity results (log_volume, amihud), which transfer better across the regime boundary, should not be assumed to generalize to every target.

**Construct dependence of the sentiment findings.** As §5.2 makes explicit, the directional conclusions depend on how sentiment is defined (tone versus price impact) and on which quantity is estimated (marginal coefficient versus group mean). This is a strength when stated carefully, but a limitation for anyone seeking a single unconditional answer: there is no framing-free statement of the asymmetry, and any restatement must carry its definitions with it.

**Corpus composition.** The news corpus is drawn from GDELT (§3.1.4), a broad machine-monitored aggregator that skews toward retail-facing web and print coverage rather than the institutional-grade feeds (trading terminals, wire services) that professional oil traders actually consume. The sentiment the models extract is therefore the sentiment of publicly available news, which may lead, lag, be biased, or simply differ from the information set that moves institutional order flow.

**Non-stationarity and single-commodity scope.** The dataset spans roughly 24 months and contains a structural break (the Iran war onset, §4.3.1) that makes the series non-stationary across the train and test boundary; the war-regime results are on a single episode and should be read as suggestive rather than established. The study is also confined to a single instrument, WTI crude oil futures. The channel decomposition and the risk-and-salience reading of the liquidity response are plausibly specific to a commodity with WTI's geopolitical supply structure, and neither the lag nor the asymmetry findings can be assumed to carry to other commodities or asset classes without replication.

## 5.5 Future work

The limitations above point to several concrete extensions, most of them small enough to be tractable.

**Small-scale human calibration.** The clearest methodological gap is the absence of human ground truth (§5.4). A tractable next step is not full-scale annotation but a small expert-annotated anchor set, a modest 200 articles scored by an oil-market professional, against which both the LLM extraction and the cross-model agreement can be benchmarked. This would turn the inter-model calibration from a standalone signal into a calibrated one, without the cost of annotating the full corpus.

**Separating tone and price sentiment as parallel features.** The construct-dependence finding (§5.2) suggests an experiment rather than only a caveat: carry both the FinBERT tone score and the LLM price-impact score as separate features and let the model weigh them. For a liquidity target in particular, where salience and risk drive volume more than price direction, the tone signal may carry information the price signal does not, and their disagreement (the geopolitical sign-flip) may itself be a useful feature marking high-uncertainty news.

**Multi-regime training.** The price_range extrapolation failure (§5.4) is a training-window problem, not a model-capacity one. As more regime episodes accumulate in the data, retraining across multiple structural breaks, or explicitly conditioning on a regime indicator, would test whether the volatility target becomes learnable once the model has seen more than one regime.

**Cross-commodity replication.** The risk-and-salience reading of the response (§5.2) is a hypothesis about oil specifically, where geopolitical supply risk is price-bullish. Replicating the two-phase design on commodities with different supply and demand structures (natural gas, gold, copper) would test whether the same risk-premium mechanism governs their news-liquidity response or whether it is specific to WTI's geopolitical exposure. Cross-commodity spillovers, oil news moving gas or gold liquidity, are a further and larger question left open here.

**Richer entity representation.** The entity flags are a binary vocabulary of 71 canonical actors (§4.3.2). Replacing them with domain-aware entity embeddings, learned representations that place, for instance, Oman and Qatar near each other as Hormuz-adjacent producers, would let the model generalize across related actors rather than treating each as an independent indicator, and could sharpen the risk-and-salience signal the current flags only approximate.
