\chapter{Background}\label{chap:background}

This chapter covers what is needed to follow the methods (Chapter~\ref{chap:methods}) and the experiments (Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}): the WTI market and how we measure its liquidity, the NLP tools that turn news into features, the Temporal Fusion Transformer, and the prior work behind the design choices made here.

\section{Terminology}\label{sec:terminology}

This section fixes the vocabulary. Each idea is picked up again where it is actually used, in Chapters~\ref{chap:methods} and~\ref{chap:experiments}.

\textbf{WTI crude oil futures and the EIA inventory cycle.} West Texas Intermediate (WTI) is the U.S.\ benchmark grade of crude oil. This thesis works on its front-month futures contract, which is the most actively traded WTI instrument. We picked WTI for three reasons: it is deeply liquid, it reacts to an event structure we can pin down (geopolitics, OPEC policy, macroeconomic releases), and it has a weekly clock. That clock is the U.S.\ Energy Information Administration (EIA) Weekly Petroleum Status Report, which publishes national crude inventory levels every Wednesday at 10:30 a.m.\ Eastern Time. It is the most regular scheduled event in the oil calendar.

\textbf{Liquidity measures.} Liquidity is how easily an asset can be traded without pushing its price around. One number will not capture that, so we use three, all computed hourly. Trading volume, in log form, is the raw activity. The Amihud illiquidity ratio \citep{amihud2002} is the absolute return per unit of volume, which tells us about price impact: how far the price moves for a given amount of trading. The Parkinson range \citep{parkinson1980} is the log high-low range within the hour, and it estimates volatility at high frequency. Between them we get activity, price impact, and volatility.

\textbf{NLP for finance.} Turning news text into numbers uses three ideas from natural language processing. Three-class sentiment classification labels a text positive, neutral, or negative, and it is what financial sentiment models normally output. Structured extraction asks the model to fill in a fixed schema instead, say a sentiment score, an event type, and a list of entities, which gives features with more than one dimension. Prompt engineering is how a large language model is told to produce that structured output, and tool-use schema enforcement is the stricter version of it, where the output shape is imposed rather than requested.

\textbf{Temporal Fusion Transformer.} The Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) of \citet{lim2021} is an attention-based neural network for multi-horizon time-series forecasting. Two parts of it matter for this thesis because they are what make it interpretable. The Variable Selection Network gives every input feature a learned importance weight. The interpretable multi-head attention layer shows how much the model looks at each past time step when it forecasts. Between them, we can read what the model is doing instead of treating it as a black box.

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related-work}

The design decisions here come from prior work rather than from first principles. This section lays out the anchors, and each one comes back at the point where it is used in Chapters~\ref{chap:methods} and~\ref{chap:experiments}.

\textbf{News sentiment and market activity.} The starting premise is that news sentiment moves trading activity and not just prices, and the finance literature already supports it. \citet{tetlock2007} found that media pessimism predicts both downward price pressure and higher trading volume. That is what justifies using news sentiment to predict hourly liquidity in Phase~1 (Chapter~\ref{chap:experiments}, Section~\ref{sec:phase1}).

\textbf{News at intraday resolution.} The reaction to news is not spread evenly across the day, it is bunched up. \citet{ederington1993} showed that scheduled macroeconomic announcements account for most of the intraday and day-of-week volatility patterns in interest-rate and foreign-exchange futures, with most of the adjustment happening within minutes of release and volatility staying high for several hours after. Despite this, most news-sentiment work in finance still runs at daily resolution, which averages away the exact structure we are trying to measure. Working hourly (Chapter~\ref{chap:methods}) is the main methodological choice that follows from this.

\textbf{News in oil markets.} At coarse resolution, oil barely reacts to news. \citet{kilian_vega2011} found no compelling response of daily energy-price changes to daily U.S.\ macroeconomic news releases, which fits the idea that energy prices are predetermined with respect to macro aggregates at a daily horizon. A null at daily frequency does not rule out an effect at finer resolution. That is part of why this thesis goes hourly, and why the target is liquidity (trading volume) rather than price.

\textbf{Financial-text sentiment models.} Transformer models adapted to the domain beat general-purpose sentiment tools on financial language. FinBERT \citep{araci2019} is the de facto baseline for financial sentiment, and we use it as the Phase~1 sentiment extractor (Section~\ref{sec:finbert}). Its output is only three classes, though, and that coarseness is exactly what motivates the richer LLM extraction in Phase~2.

\textbf{Liquidity measurement.} Hourly liquidity is measured with standard market-microstructure quantities: trading volume, the Parkinson high-low range \citep{parkinson1980}, and the Amihud illiquidity ratio \citep{amihud2002}, which captures price impact per unit of traded volume.

\textbf{Oil-price shock decomposition.} An oil-market move is not one undifferentiated shock. It is a mix of drivers that can be separated, and the canonical way of separating them is the structural decomposition of the real oil price into supply, aggregate-demand, and precautionary (risk) demand components \citep{kilian2009}. This is the basis for the Phase~2 channel decomposition (Sections~\ref{sec:channel-decomposition} and~\ref{sec:llm-extraction}), which pulls a \texttt{supply\_impact}, a \texttt{demand\_impact}, and a \texttt{risk\_premium} out of each article rather than squashing them onto one sentiment axis. It also explains why supply-risk geopolitical news is price-bullish rather than bearish.

\textbf{Interpretable multi-horizon forecasting.} The Temporal Fusion Transformer \citep{lim2021} forecasts over multiple horizons while staying interpretable, through its Variable Selection Network and its interpretable multi-head attention. Those two mechanisms are what let Phase~2 (Sections~\ref{sec:tft-methods}, \ref{sec:tft-v1}, and~\ref{sec:tft-v2}) read feature importance and temporal attention straight off the model. We use them directly to corroborate the lag structure in RQ1.

\textbf{LLM-based extraction and cross-model validation.} Large language models can pull structured, multi-dimensional features out of financial text. They can also stand in for annotation work that would otherwise be expensive: \citet{gilardi2023} found they match or beat crowd workers on text labelling, and \citet{zheng2023} used them to evaluate other models' outputs. Phase~2 leans on both. Claude Haiku extracts the schema in Section~\ref{sec:schema}, giving the richer, price-oriented, channel-decomposed features that FinBERT's three-class tone label cannot produce. The inter-model calibration of Section~\ref{sec:calibration-methods} (Section~\ref{sec:calibration}) then validates those features by checking them against a second model.
